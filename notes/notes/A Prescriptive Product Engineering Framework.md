

## PHASE 0 — Company Intent (Non-negotiable Constraints)

### 0.1 Company Mission

**Why the company exists today.**

- Short, stable, inspirational
- Does not change frequently
- Guides prioritization in ambiguous situations

### 0.2 Company Vision

**What the world looks like if the mission succeeds.**

- Long-term
- Directional, not measurable

### 0.3 Company Goals

**Measurable outcomes the company must achieve.**

- Business (revenue, growth, sustainability)
- Impact (users helped, efficiency gained)
- Strategic (market position, moat)

> Output of Phase 0:  
> **A fixed context** within which all products must operate.

---

## PHASE 1 — Problem Selection & Discovery (Build the Right Thing)

### 1.1 Define the Problem Space

Choose a **specific domain and user segment** that meaningfully advances the company mission.

- Who exactly is the user?
- In what context do they operate?
- What constraints exist (time, skill, regulation, environment)?

> Avoid: “Education”, “AI”, “Productivity”  
> Prefer: “High school teachers creating formative assessments under time pressure”

---

### 1.2 User & Domain Research

Understand the reality before proposing solutions.

Activities may include:

- User interviews / observation
- Jobs-to-be-Done analysis
- Current workflow mapping
- Pain frequency vs severity analysis
- Existing alternatives(reverse engineer existing products) and failure points 

You should be able to clearly articulate:

> “Users struggle with **X** when trying to achieve **Y** because of **Z**.”

If this sentence is weak, stop here.

---

### 1.3 Problem Validation

Ensure the problem is worth solving.

Validate:

- Is the pain real and recurring?
- Do users actively try to solve it today?
- Is the problem aligned with business goals?
- Is it feasible for _this_ company to solve?

> Output of Phase 1:  
> **A validated, well-scoped problem statement**

---

## PHASE 2 — Product Intent (Why This Product Exists)

### 2.1 Product Mission

**Why this product exists in one sentence.**

- Narrow
- User-focused
- Stable across versions

Example:

> “Enable teachers to create meaningful, adaptive assignments in minutes.”

---

### 2.2 Product Vision

**What success looks like if the product mission is fulfilled.**

- Inspirational
- Guides long-term decisions
- Helps say “no” to misaligned features
    

---

### 2.3 Product Success Metrics

Define **how value will be measured**.

- User success metrics (task completion time, adoption, retention)
- Business metrics (activation, conversion, cost reduction)
- Quality metrics (reliability, trust, satisfaction)
    

> Output of Phase 2:  
> **Clarity on purpose and how success will be judged**

---

## PHASE 3 — Outcome-Driven Roadmap (Hypotheses, Not Promises)

### 3.1 Define Desired Outcomes

Break the vision into **measurable user or business outcomes**.

Examples:

- Teachers can publish an assignment in under 10 minutes    
- Students complete assignments with fewer drop-offs
- Content reuse increases across cohorts
    

---

### 3.2 Roadmap as Hypotheses

Each roadmap item should state:

- The outcome we want
- The user behavior we expect to change
- The assumption we are testing
    

Format:

> “We believe that **[change]** will result in **[outcome]** because **[reason]**.”

---

### 3.3 Prioritization

Prioritize based on:

- Impact on core problem
- Learning value
- Effort and risk
- Strategic alignment

> Output of Phase 3:  
> **A flexible roadmap oriented around outcomes and learning**

---

## PHASE 4 — Epics: Coherent Value Slices

### 4.1 Define Epics

Each epic represents a **complete, user-perceivable unit of value**.

Characteristics:

- Solves a meaningful sub-problem
- Can be validated independently
- Delivers value even if later work is cancelled
    

Example:

> “Teachers can assemble multi-step interactive assignments.”

---

### 4.2 Epic Success Criteria

For each epic, define:

- Expected user behavior change
- Metrics that indicate success
- Qualitative signals (feedback, usability)

> Output of Phase 4:  
> **A set of value-aligned epics with clear success definitions**

---

## PHASE 5 — User Stories: User Intent and Value

### 5.1 Define User Stories

User stories capture **user intent**, not tasks.

Format:

> As a **[user]**, I want to **[achieve outcome]**, so that **[value]**.

Rules:

- Story must be testable via user behavior
- Must belong clearly to one epic
- Must be understandable without technical context

---

### 5.2 Acceptance Criteria

Define what “done” means from the user’s perspective.

- Functional expectations
- Edge cases
- Constraints
    

> Output of Phase 5:  
> **A backlog grounded in user value**

---

## PHASE 6 — Technical Design & Sub-Tasks (Build It Right)

### 6.1 Technical Decomposition

Break stories into:

- Architecture decisions
- Data models
- API contracts
- Frontend/backend tasks
- Non-functional requirements
    

---

### 6.2 Engineering Quality Constraints

Define:

- Performance expectations
- Security/privacy requirements
- Maintainability standards
- Observability needs
    

> Output of Phase 6:  
> **An implementation plan that serves user value, not the other way around**

---

## PHASE 7 — Delivery, Validation, and Learning Loops

### 7.1 Incremental Delivery

Release in small, testable increments.

---

### 7.2 Measure and Observe

After release:

- Compare actual behavior vs expected outcomes    
- Track defined success metrics
- Collect qualitative feedback

---

### 7.3 Learn, Adjust, Decide

For each epic or roadmap item:

- Double down
- Iterate
- Pivot
- Kill

This is **mandatory** for avoiding waste.

> Output of Phase 7:  
> **Validated learning and continuous improvement**

---

## PHASE 8 — Continuous Alignment & Evolution

- Revisit assumptions regularly
- Adjust roadmap based on learning
- Ensure ongoing alignment with company mission    

---

## Summary (Mental Model)

**Intent → Problem → Product Purpose → Outcomes → Value Slices → User Intent → Technical Execution → Learning**
